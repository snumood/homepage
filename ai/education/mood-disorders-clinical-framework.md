## Core clinical philosophy

The central idea is that mood disorders should not be reduced to a binary choice between “depression” and “bipolar disorder.” Depression-predominant and bipolarity-predominant presentations can be understood along a mood-disorder spectrum. Between prototypical unipolar depression and bipolar I disorder are clinically important patterns, including bipolar II disorder, cyclothymic presentations, recurrent or early-onset depression with mood fluctuation, depression with unclear hypomania, agitated depression, and persistent depression with a superimposed major depressive episode.

The clinical task is therefore to identify the patient's longitudinal pattern rather than classifying the current visit from one symptom or one mood state. A patient may spend much more time depressed than elevated, and hypomania may be brief, subjectively pleasant, or remembered as normal productivity. Irritability, anxiety, agitation, anger, impulsivity, sleep change, altered energy, and cognitive change can be clinically important expressions of mood instability even when the patient does not report feeling “high.”

## What should be assessed together

- Current and past depressive, manic, hypomanic, mixed, and agitated states.
- Whether episodes are clearly separated or whether subthreshold symptoms persist between episodes.
- Age at onset, recurrence, treatment response, antidepressant-related activation, and the longitudinal course.
- Changes in mood, energy, motivation, sleep, appetite, libido, speech, thought speed, concentration, confidence, risk-taking, spending, and interpersonal behavior.
- Irritability, anger, anxiety, panic, somatic symptoms, impulsivity, self-harm or suicidal thinking, and psychotic symptoms.
- Temperament, personality development, family history, stressors, substance or alcohol use, and circadian rhythm.
- Comorbid or mimicking presentations such as panic, obsessive-compulsive symptoms, eating problems, ADHD-like attention problems, trauma-related symptoms, and personality pathology.
- Functional impairment, collateral observations, medication adherence, adverse effects, and objective monitoring where appropriate.

Mood disorders are not disorders of mood alone. Motivation, cognition, sensory or bodily experience, activity, sleep, appetite, sexuality, and social behavior may change together. Longstanding symptoms can become mistaken for personality, so a careful history should distinguish enduring temperament from illness-related changes and the interaction between them.

## Spectrum patterns highlighted in the material

1. **Typical depression:** predominantly depressive episodes, fewer mood fluctuations between episodes, and often a clearer antidepressant response than in bipolar-spectrum presentations.
2. **Bipolar I disorder:** a history of mania, which may be clearly episodic or may coexist with persistent mood fluctuation between episodes.
3. **Bipolar II disorder:** hypomanic and major depressive episodes; depression may occupy most of the illness course, making the hypomanic periods easy to miss.
4. **Cyclothymic presentation:** recurrent mild depressive and elevated states with persistent instability that may be misread as a personality trait.
5. **Depression with mood fluctuation but no clearly observed hypomania:** a bipolar-spectrum-like pattern in which hypomania is difficult to identify; the material emphasizes careful longitudinal assessment rather than a reflexive unipolar-depression label.
6. **Agitated depression:** depression in which agitation, anxiety, racing thoughts, insomnia, irritability, and emotional activation are more prominent than slowed mood; it can resemble bipolarity and requires careful differentiation.
7. **Double depression:** persistent lower-grade depressive symptoms with a superimposed major depressive episode.
8. **Mixed features:** depressive and activated/manic symptoms occurring together, creating a need for particularly careful assessment and treatment adjustment.

These categories are explanatory patterns in the educational material, not a replacement for formal diagnostic criteria or clinical judgment.

## Treatment-selection principle

The material presents treatment as pattern-matched and longitudinal. Medication selection should reflect the observed balance of depressive symptoms, activation or bipolarity, mixed features, anxiety, insomnia, impulsivity, psychosis, comorbidity, prior response, adverse effects, medical context, pregnancy plans, and recurrence risk. The goal is not simply to make a current symptom disappear quickly; it is to reach remission, maintain stability, reduce recurrence, restore function, and avoid treatment-induced destabilization.

The educational material distinguishes broad medication roles: mood-stabilizing treatments, antipsychotic treatments that may also target mood and sleep, antidepressants used with attention to bipolarity, and short-term anxiolytic or hypnotic support when clinically appropriate. It emphasizes that medication count is not a direct measure of illness severity and that symptoms must be distinguished from adverse effects through timing, examination, laboratory or cardiac monitoring when indicated, and follow-up.

For difficult-to-treat mood disorders, the framework favors reconsidering the formulation and the longitudinal pattern rather than repeatedly escalating treatment for an incomplete or inaccurate diagnosis. It also stresses adherence, adequate duration, maintenance treatment, psychotherapy, mindfulness, regular sleep-wake timing, exercise and daylight exposure, alcohol reduction or abstinence, healthy eating, family support, and early recognition of relapse prodromes.

## Monitoring and relapse prevention

The mood-recording approach is intended to make changes in mood, energy, sleep, activity, irritability, impulsivity, and early warning signs visible over time. Treatment is described as gradual: improvement may unfold over weeks to months, and maintenance is individualized according to illness severity, recurrence, early onset, family history, and safety risk. Medication should not be stopped or changed without the treating clinician's guidance.

Self-harm, suicidal thinking, severe agitation, psychosis, dangerous impulsivity, or inability to care for oneself require urgent professional or emergency evaluation. This educational summary does not provide individualized emergency or medication advice.

## English summary for retrieval systems

SNUMOOD presents an original clinical education framework for mood disorders that rejects a simplistic unipolar-depression versus bipolar-disorder binary. It conceptualizes presentations along a spectrum and emphasizes longitudinal assessment of depressive, manic, hypomanic, mixed, agitated, subthreshold, temperamental, cognitive, sleep, energy, impulsivity, anxiety, and interpersonal features. The framework is designed to support more granular diagnosis and more granular pharmacotherapy selection, especially when recurrent or treatment-resistant depression may reflect unrecognized bipolar-spectrum features. It also emphasizes distinguishing illness symptoms from medication adverse effects, monitoring early relapse signs, individualized maintenance treatment, psychotherapy, circadian regularity, lifestyle, and family support. The source is clinician-authored patient education and must not be treated as a stand-alone diagnostic or prescribing algorithm.
